to get started...

With cameraphones, digital photography became more accessible to the average consumer than ever before; the constantly-improving capabilities of the modern-day smartphone, in conjunction with the always-increasing ways for us to capture and store pictures, has led to the average consumer possessing more photographs than traditional digital folder structures can efficiently and effectively manage.

To establish the need for this project, I observed the photo management habits of several millenials, some of which relied on their phones, some of which relied on their computers, and some of which used the cloud the manage both. All of these mechanisms relied on taxonomic and heirarchal folders, but the setups observed were never fully in-sync with how participants mentally organized their photographs.

1000 Words was born out of a desire to make photo management more engaging and more seamless, because we can never have too many ways to do the same thing.

Taxonomies can be too arbitrary to be useful, while folksonomies are often difficult to populate and consistently apply. 1000 Words is an attempt at balancing the two.

so in the end...

By leveraging existing technologies, frameworks, and APIs, 1000 Words not only tries to get users started by pre-populating data that it has permissible access to, such as location data and calendar events, but also prompts users to fill in the gaps and correct mislabeled data by turning each picture into an actual story.

The use of this Mad Libs format shifts the process from being about categorizing the picture to being about describing the picture. Attributes from that description, from where and when it was taken to who and what is in the picture, become tags that can be used by social networks and other picture sharing systems; these tags also become how users search for past pictures.

The heirarchies that people followed usually had to do with places and times; by making use of metadata and bolstering that with other commonly-used bits of information, users can create structures that are just as specific and heirarchal as their desktop folder structures without being limited to one schema of such.

 

A picture tells a thousand words.

1000 words is all it takes to manage all your memories.

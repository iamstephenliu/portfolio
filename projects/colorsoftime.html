to get started...

With Colors of Time, we set out to find ways to change how people perceive and interact with their time. The intention with this project was always to find a way to bring tactile interactions into peoples' relationship with their calendars, intially with the assumption that hands-on manipulation of elements would help make scheduling a day, or even a week, less daunting and mentally taxing.

We began a round of research to investigate whether or not a tactile calendar board that used pop-up tiles and LEDs to signal when events and meetings are scheduled would fulfill this need. Whereas concepts such as the LEGO wall calendar provide a tactile and cloud-connected calendar solution, it fails to function two-ways: changes made in the cloud aren't reflected on the LEGO board, hence our use of pop-up tiles that could be manipulated by hand or remotely.

As it turns out, notebooks formatted as to-do lists was a common form of time management among interview subjects, requiring a pivot.

so in the end...

As a result, we began to investigate whether or not proving an artistic and abstracted representation of one's schedule would be able to make personal calendars less intimidating and more gratifying.

One of the concepts that we investigated was relying on a tower of split-flap displays; each row would be of a single, distinct color tied to a to-do or event/meeting item within a user's Google calendar, and as each event/meeting was completed and as each to-do item was cross off, the bottom-most row of the split-flap tower would clear out and the remaining colors would cascade down.

The use of colors instead of text was intended to facilitate flexibility and abstraction, while a split-flap mechanism was chosen in order to utilize the sounds of the mechanism to evoke feelings of motion and accomplishment.

As a result, we pivoted again towards other elements that have associations with time. We decided to use pendulums that would draw upon a sheet of paper. The movement of the pendulums harkens to grandfather clocks, and the movement of brushes against paper results in a sweeping sound intended to be associated with the movement and passage of time.

We built out a mock-up of the installation, with an Arduino powering the rotating base that holds the paper. The idea is for the pendulums to be equipped with motors that would move each pendulum to a height that based on the number of activities and events on a given day in someone's calendar. The resulting piece is intended to be an abstract and drawn representation of a person's schedule: a visual artpiece meant to impart a new and lighter meaning to one of peoples' biggest stressors.
